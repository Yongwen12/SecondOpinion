# 标注手册

SecondOpinion IAA Pilot Annotation Codebook (v0.1)

## 0. Why This Pilot Exists

Before spending money on 200-300 expert labels, we need to answer one question:

> Is "reviewer-claim robustness" a construct that independent human experts can agree on, and is it the same thing as the lifecycle-robustness score the system already computes?

The current pseudo-expert harness shows about 12% exact agreement with system-expected labels and 100% agreement on evidence-support. Those two numbers together are a warning, not a result: one dimension is near-random, the other is near-tautological because both sides read the same evidence score. We cannot tell whether the system is wrong, the schema is incoherent, or the task is irreducibly subjective.

This pilot is the cheapest experiment that distinguishes those three cases. It does not try to label many papers. It labels a small set with full overlap across at least 3 annotators, so we can measure human-human agreement before human-system agreement.

Decision this pilot drives: scale to full expert labeling (green), revise and re-pilot (yellow), or redefine the metric entirely (red). See Section 8.

## 1. The Two Constructs

Every reviewer claim is judged along two different axes. The point of the pilot is that these may not be the same thing, and the product eventually has to pick one as its target.

**Construct A - Claim epistemic quality (normative).**
Would a careful domain expert consider this a well-founded, material, fair concern about the paper, judged on its own merits and independent of what happened to it later? This is what a human annotator provides and what the system cannot compute on its own.

**Construct B - Claim lifecycle influence (descriptive).**
Did the concern survive and propagate: grounded in the review -> supported by other reviewers -> not resolved by rebuttal -> discussed after rebuttal -> taken up in the meta-review? This is mechanically computed by the system. Annotators do not label it.

Hypothesis under test: A and B are correlated but distinct. A strong claim can be ignored by a tired AC (high A, low B); a weak claim can be parroted into a meta-review (low A, high B). The correlation between human-A and system-B is an empirical output of this pilot (Section 9), and it determines what "auditing review quality" can honestly claim.

## 2. What Annotators Label

Annotators label normative judgments only a domain expert can give:

- Whether the claim is correct about the paper (grounding accuracy).
- Whether the claim matters if correct (materiality).
- The holistic quality of the claim.
- Whether the rebuttal actually resolved it.
- What the author should do about it.

Annotators do not label system-computed lifecycle signals:

- Whether the concern appears in the meta-review or survived the lifecycle (Construct B).
- The final accept/reject decision.
- Any system score.

Keeping humans on the normative axis only is the main cost control: it lets one annotator judge a claim from a short evidence packet instead of re-reviewing the whole paper.

## 3. Annotation Unit

One record equals one reviewer claim. For each claim, the annotator is shown:

1. Paper title and abstract.
2. The paper passages the system retrieved as evidence for this claim, not the full PDF.
3. The reviewer claim text and its location: review id, field, and character offsets.
4. The author rebuttal segment(s) addressing this claim, or an explicit "no rebuttal found" marker.

The following are deliberately hidden from the annotator to prevent leakage and mirror the closed-book RAG discipline:

- The meta-review and final decision.
- Whether other reviewers raised the same point.
- The system's own scores and labels.

Insufficient-evidence rule: if the provided passages do not contain enough to judge a dimension, the annotator marks it `cant_tell` and sets `evidence_sufficient=false` rather than guessing. That flag is data: it measures gaps in the system's evidence retrieval, and those claims are excluded from agreement stats.

## 4. Label Schema

All scales are ordinal where possible, so we can use weighted agreement metrics. Annotators consider one claim at a time.

### 4.1 `grounding_accuracy`

Is the claim correct about the paper?

This is different from the system's "grounding in the review." The system checks whether the claim traces back to review text. Here the human checks whether the claim is factually right about the manuscript.

- `accurate`: correctly characterizes what the paper does or says.
- `partial`: partly right but overstates, omits a relevant caveat, or mixes a correct and an incorrect point.
- `inaccurate`: misreads the paper, for example asks for something the paper already provides.
- `cant_tell`: provided evidence is insufficient to judge.

### 4.2 `materiality`

If the concern is valid, how much does it matter?

- `critical`: undermines the paper's central claim or contribution.
- `major`: affects a substantial result or the strength of the conclusions.
- `minor`: affects a secondary result, clarity, or completeness.
- `cosmetic`: typo-level, stylistic, or out-of-scope for this paper's contribution.

### 4.3 `claim_quality`

Holistic normative verdict and the headline of Construct A.

Consider grounding, materiality, fairness, and scope. For example: is the reviewer moving goalposts, demanding out-of-scope experiments, or imposing personal preference?

- `high`: well-founded, material, fair; the kind of concern the process should reward.
- `borderline`: defensible but weak, vague, or partly unfair.
- `low`: unfounded, purely subjective, vague-without-content, or out-of-scope.

### 4.4 `rebuttal_resolution`

Did the author response substantively address this claim?

This dimension directly tests the system finding that many rebuttals are lexically responsive but rarely resolve the reviewer concern.

- `fully_resolves`: provides the requested evidence or argument, and the concern no longer stands.
- `partially`: addresses part of the concern, or weakens but does not remove it.
- `deflects_generic`: acknowledges, thanks, promises camera-ready changes, or restates without substantive engagement.
- `not_addressed`: the rebuttal does not engage this claim at all, though a rebuttal exists elsewhere.
- `no_rebuttal`: no author response to this claim.

### 4.5 `recommended_action`

What should the author prioritize?

This tests the product's recommended author action. Current pseudo-expert agreement is about 38%.

- `must_address`: answering this is necessary to have a shot at acceptance.
- `should_address`: important; answer if at all possible.
- `optional`: worth a brief reply, not decisive.
- `deprioritize`: safe to skip or give a one-line reply.

### 4.6 `annotator_confidence`

The annotator's own confidence in their labels for this claim:

- `high`
- `medium`
- `low`

Use this to separate confident disagreement, where the construct is genuinely contested, from low-confidence disagreement, where more evidence or guidance may be needed. Report IAA both with and without low-confidence items.

### 4.7 `rationale`

One to three sentences explaining why these labels were chosen. This field is required.

The rationale is the most valuable field for adjudication and for understanding why humans disagree, which is itself a core product insight.

## 5. JSONL Record Schema

One line per claim-annotator pair, matching the existing `data/validation/*.jsonl` convention:

```json
{
  "claim_id": "string",
  "paper_id": "string",
  "review_id": "string",
  "annotator_id": "string",
  "grounding_accuracy": "accurate | partial | inaccurate | cant_tell",
  "materiality": "critical | major | minor | cosmetic",
  "claim_quality": "high | borderline | low",
  "rebuttal_resolution": "fully_resolves | partially | deflects_generic | not_addressed | no_rebuttal",
  "recommended_action": "must_address | should_address | optional | deprioritize",
  "annotator_confidence": "high | medium | low",
  "evidence_sufficient": true,
  "rationale": "string",
  "timestamp": "ISO-8601"
}
```

Keep the system's computed Construct-B fields in a separate file keyed by `claim_id`, joined only at analysis time (Section 9). Never show them to annotators.

## 6. Calibration Round

Run this before the scored pilot. Inter-annotator agreement on subjective tasks rises sharply with a short calibration pass. Do not skip this.

1. All annotators independently label the same 2-3 papers, about 30-50 claims.
2. Hold a reconciliation meeting: walk every disagreement, decide the intended label, and add a worked example to this codebook for each label level that caused confusion.
3. Freeze codebook as v0.2.
4. Only then run the scored pilot (Section 7) on a fresh, disjoint set of papers.

Calibration-round labels are not counted in the reported IAA because they are contaminated by the discussion.

## 7. Pilot Logistics

- Annotators: at least 3, domain = ML, ideally people who have reviewed for ICLR, NeurIPS, or similar venues. Two is not enough because you cannot distinguish "hard task" from "one outlier."
- Papers: 15-20, disjoint from the calibration set, drawn from the same ICLR 2024 calibration pool already available.
- Unit count: at about 10-15 claims per paper, this yields about 200-300 claim-level judgments per annotator, enough for a pilot.
- Overlap: full overlap. All annotators label all claims. Full overlap is required to compute IAA; partial overlap is for the later cost-optimized scale phase.
- Blinding: meta-review, decision, and system scores hidden (Section 3).
- Effort estimate: about 3-5 minutes per claim after calibration, or roughly 45-75 person-hours for 300 claims x 3 annotators. This is roughly one-tenth the cost of the full 200-300-paper labeling effort under consideration. The goal is to pay 10% to find out whether the other 90% is worth spending.

## 8. Agreement Metrics and Go/No-Go Thresholds

Compute agreement per dimension, not just as an aggregate. Expect `grounding_accuracy` to agree well and `materiality` / `recommended_action` to agree worst.

- Ordinal dimensions (`grounding_accuracy`, `materiality`, `claim_quality`, `recommended_action`): use Krippendorff's alpha (ordinal) or quadratic-weighted Fleiss/Cohen kappa. Weighted metrics matter because "critical vs major" is a smaller error than "critical vs cosmetic."
- Categorical dimension (`rebuttal_resolution`): use Fleiss' kappa for 3+ raters.
- Report each metric twice: all items, and excluding `low` confidence / `evidence_sufficient=false` items.

The primary gate is on `claim_quality`, the headline of Construct A. Thresholds below follow common Krippendorff and Landis-Koch conventions. They are rules of thumb, not laws, but for a subjective construct, reaching them is itself meaningful.

| Result on `claim_quality` | Reading | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| alpha >= 0.67 | Construct is reliable enough to treat as gold | GREEN: scale to full expert labeling; use as gold for system validation, RAG target, and only then any fine-tuning data |
| 0.40 <= alpha < 0.67 | Marginal / contested | YELLOW: revise codebook, add anchors, run one more calibration + pilot loop. Do not scale yet |
| alpha < 0.40 | Humans do not agree, so there is no gold to chase | RED: stop. Redefine the metric, likely narrow to the sub-dimensions that did agree, such as `grounding_accuracy`, and drop the holistic robustness claim |

A red result is not a failure of the pilot. It is the highest-value thing the pilot can tell us, and it costs about 10% of finding it out the expensive way.

## 9. Cross-Analysis

This experiment answers the Construct-A-vs-B question from Section 1.

1. Take the adjudicated human `claim_quality` label (Construct A, gold).
2. Join, by `claim_id`, the system's lifecycle-robustness score (Construct B).
3. Compute their correlation: Spearman rho, plus a confusion matrix of high/borderline/low quality x high/medium/low robustness.

Interpretation:

- Strong correlation: quality and influence largely coincide. Lifecycle robustness is a defensible proxy for claim quality, and the product story holds as written.
- Weak or zero correlation: the pilot has empirically confirmed conflation. The system is measuring whether a concern was influential, not whether it was good. The product target must then be chosen explicitly:
  - Author-facing triage wants Construct B: what will actually move the AC.
  - Reviewer/venue quality audit wants Construct A: whether the criticism was well-founded.
  - Product language must market exactly that, not both.

Also report system-vs-human agreement on `rebuttal_resolution` and `recommended_action`. These validate or refute the two headline product features directly.

## 10. Adjudication and Gold Creation

- A senior adjudicator, ideally a fourth person who did not annotate, resolves each disagreement to a single gold label.
- For every adjudicated disagreement, log a reason code: `genuine_ambiguity`, `annotator_error`, `guideline_gap`, or `evidence_gap`.
- The distribution of reason codes is a deliverable. Many `guideline_gap` labels mean the issue is fixable with a better codebook. Many `genuine_ambiguity` labels mean the construct itself is soft, and the Section 8 red logic may apply even if alpha is borderline.

## Appendix A - Worked Examples

Illustrative ML-review claims for calibration. These examples are fabricated, not drawn from real papers.

### A1. Grounded, Material, Fair

Claim: "The main convergence theorem assumes bounded gradients, but the experiments use unclipped Adam, so the guarantee does not cover the reported runs."

Recommended labels:

- `grounding_accuracy`: `accurate`
- `materiality`: `major`
- `claim_quality`: `high`
- `rebuttal_resolution`: `fully_resolves`, if the author adds a clipped-gradient run resolving it
- `recommended_action`: `must_address`

### A2. Vague Novelty Concern

Claim: "The paper is not novel enough and the contribution feels incremental."

Recommended labels:

- `grounding_accuracy`: `cant_tell`
- `materiality`: `major`
- `claim_quality`: `low`
- `recommended_action`: `should_address`

Rationale: vague, no specific grounding; low quality despite a serious-sounding topic.

### A3. Reviewer Misreading

Claim: "The method only works for convex objectives," when Section 4 explicitly handles the non-convex case.

Recommended labels:

- `grounding_accuracy`: `inaccurate`
- `materiality`: `minor`, because the error is the reviewer's
- `claim_quality`: `low`
- `recommended_action`: `should_address`, because the author should politely correct the misreading

### A4. Generic Rebuttal

Rebuttal: reviewer asks for a specific missing baseline; author replies only "thank you, we will add this in the camera-ready."

Recommended label:

- `rebuttal_resolution`: `deflects_generic`

Rationale: lexically responsive, substantively unresolved. This is exactly the pattern the system flags.

### A5. Resolving Rebuttal

Rebuttal: author provides the requested ablation table in-thread showing the effect is robust.

Recommended label:

- `rebuttal_resolution`: `fully_resolves`
